What is the electric flux through a closed Gaussian surface enclosing an electric dipole of charge $-q$ and $+q$?

A) Zero
B) $\frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$
C) $\frac{2q}{\varepsilon_0}$
D) $\frac{q}{2\varepsilon_0}$

### Solution
According to Gauss's Law, the total electric flux $\Phi$ through any closed surface is equal to the net charge $Q_{enclosed}$ divided by the permittivity of free space $\varepsilon_0$:
$$\Phi = \frac{Q_{enclosed}}{\varepsilon_0}$$
An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges, $-q$ and $+q$. The net charge enclosed by the Gaussian surface is:
$$Q_{enclosed} = +q + (-q) = 0$$
Hence, the total electric flux is zero.

If the temperature of a metallic conductor increases, how do its resistance ($R$) and relaxation time ($\tau$) change?

A) Both $R$ and $\tau$ increase
B) Both $R$ and $\tau$ decrease
C) $R$ increases while $\tau$ decreases
D) $R$ decreases while $\tau$ increases

### Solution
Resistance $R$ is related to relaxation time $\tau$ by the formula:
$$R = \frac{m l}{n e^2 \tau A}$$
As temperature increases, the thermal energy of free electrons increases, causing more frequent collisions with lattice ions. This decreases the average relaxation time ($\tau$) between collisions. Since resistance is inversely proportional to relaxation time ($R \propto 1/\tau$), decreasing $\tau$ results in an increase in resistance $R$.

A proton and an alpha particle enter a uniform magnetic field perpendicularly with the same kinetic energy. What is the ratio of the radii of their circular paths ($r_p : r_\alpha$)?

A) 1 : 1
B) 1 : 2
C) 2 : 1
D) 1 : 4

### Solution
The radius $r$ of the circular path of a charged particle in a magnetic field is given by:
$$r = \frac{m v}{q B} = \frac{\sqrt{2 m K}}{q B}$$
where $K$ is the kinetic energy, $m$ is the mass, and $q$ is the charge.
Given they have the same kinetic energy $K$ and magnetic field $B$:
$$\frac{r_p}{r_\alpha} = \frac{\sqrt{m_p}}{q_p} \cdot \frac{q_\alpha}{\sqrt{m_\alpha}}$$
We know that:
- $m_\alpha = 4 m_p$
- $q_\alpha = 2 q_p$
Substituting these:
$$\frac{r_p}{r_\alpha} = \frac{\sqrt{m_p}}{q_p} \cdot \frac{2 q_p}{\sqrt{4 m_p}} = \frac{\sqrt{m_p}}{1} \cdot \frac{2}{2 \sqrt{m_p}} = 1$$
Therefore, $r_p : r_\alpha = 1:1$.

What happens to the width of the central maximum in a single slit diffraction pattern if the width of the slit is doubled?

A) It becomes halved
B) It becomes doubled
C) It remains unchanged
D) It becomes four times

### Solution
The angular width of the central maximum in single-slit diffraction is given by:
$$2\theta = \frac{2\lambda}{a}$$
where $\lambda$ is the wavelength and $a$ is the slit width.
If the slit width $a$ is doubled ($a' = 2a$), the new angular width is:
$$2\theta' = \frac{2\lambda}{2a} = \frac{1}{2} (2\theta)$$
Therefore, the width of the central maximum becomes halved.

According to Einstein's photoelectric equation, the graph plotted between the stopping potential ($V_0$) and frequency ($\nu$) of incident radiation is a straight line whose slope is:

A) $h$
B) $e$
C) $h e$
D) $\frac{h}{e}$

### Solution
Einstein's photoelectric equation is:
$$e V_0 = h \nu - \phi_0 \implies V_0 = \left(\frac{h}{e}\right)\nu - \frac{\phi_0}{e}$$
Comparing this with the equation of a straight line $y = m x + c$:
- $y = V_0$
- $x = \nu$
- Slope $m = \frac{h}{e}$
- Intercept $c = -\frac{\phi_0}{e}$
Thus, the slope of the graph is $\frac{h}{e}$.

A step-up transformer operates on a 220 V line and supplies a current of 2 A to a secondary load. If the ratio of the primary to secondary turns ($N_p : N_s$) is 1 : 10, calculate the electrical current flowing in the primary coil (in Amperes), assuming 100% efficiency.

### Solution
For a 100% efficient transformer:
$$\frac{I_p}{I_s} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$$
Given $I_s = 2 \text{ A}$ and $\frac{N_p}{N_s} = \frac{1}{10} \implies \frac{N_s}{N_p} = 10$:
$$\frac{I_p}{2} = 10 \implies I_p = 20 \text{ A}$$

Calculate the de Broglie wavelength (in Angstroms, $\text{Å}$) of an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 100 V.

### Solution
The de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential $V$ is given by:
$$\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å}$$
Substitute $V = 100 \text{ V}$:
$$\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{12.27}{10} = 1.227 \text{ Å}$$
The accepted range is 1.2 to 1.3 Å.

How does the binding energy per nucleon change as the mass number $A$ increases from light nuclei to heavy nuclei?

A) It increases continuously
B) It decreases continuously
C) It first increases, reaches a maximum around $A \approx 56$, and then decreases slowly
D) It remains constant for all nuclei

### Solution
The binding energy per nucleon curve shows that:
1. For very light nuclei (like hydrogen, helium), it is low.
2. It increases with mass number $A$ and reaches a maximum value of about 8.8 MeV per nucleon for Iron ($^{56}\text{Fe}$).
3. Beyond $A = 56$, it decreases gradually due to increasing electrostatic repulsion between protons.
This explain why fusion occurs in lighter nuclei and fission in heavier ones.
